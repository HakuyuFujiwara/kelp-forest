Why this kit

Three reasons this is more than another beach diorama.

01

You actually make something.

Other kits show kelp. This kit transforms it. You'll mix, color, and cross-link real bioplastic you can hold in your hand by the end of class.

02

It connects to live research.

Scientists at AltaSea (right here at the Port of LA) are studying how kelp can replace fossil-fuel plastic. You're trying their question on a kitchen scale.

03

It's the future, not a dream.

Kelp grows up to 2 feet a day, soaks up CO₂, and breaks down naturally. Your bioplastic is one possible answer to plastic pollution.

The experiment

Seven steps from seaweed to plastic.

Tap Done as you finish each step. Use the timer when one appears. There's no race — slow steps are where the science happens.

Bioplastic Field Protocol
Step 1 of 7
01Mise en place

Set up your station.

Lay out everything from the materials list. Goggles on. Phone nearby for the timer (this page works as one). A clear table is a happy table.

Why it mattersReal labs work in a clean, ordered space because chemistry is fast — once you start, you can't stop to find the spoon.
setup ✦
02Hydrate the alginate

Whisk 2 g sodium alginate into 100 mL water.

Add the alginate slowly while whisking. It clumps if you dump it. Whisk for 5 minutes — your arm should get a little tired. Then cover and rest for 30 minutes. Use that time to read up on how giant kelp builds the food web below.

Why it mattersSodium alginate is a long molecule pulled from kelp cell walls. In water it uncoils and forms a gel — the base of every step that follows.
5:00
Whisking timer 5 minutes of vigorous mixing.
stir ↻
03Add the glycerin

Stir in 1.5 mL glycerin.

Glycerin is the difference between a brittle plastic and a bendy one. Add it now and stir gently for about a minute. If you've ever held a thin, flexible plastic bag, that's glycerin's doing.

Why it mattersGlycerin is a plasticizer. It slips between the alginate strands and lets them slide past each other — turning rigid into rubbery.
+1.5 mL
04Color it (your call)

Add 2–3 drops of food coloring.

Skip if you want clear plastic. Two drops of one color makes a pastel sheet; three of two colors makes marble. This is the only step where there's a wrong answer only if you don't have fun with it.

Why it mattersReal bioplastic R&D includes pigments too — companies tint kelp packaging exactly the way you're about to.
choose ✿
05Pour the gel

Spread a thin layer on your dish or mold.

Aim for about 3 mm thick. Thinner = drier faster but more fragile. Thicker = stronger but takes longer to dry. Tilt the dish gently to even it out. Pop a few bubbles with a toothpick.

Why it mattersSurface area controls the cross-linking reaction. The thinner the layer, the faster the calcium ions can reach every alginate strand.
~3mm
06The magic moment

Spray with calcium chloride solution.

Mix 5 g calcium chloride into 100 mL water in your spray bottle. Mist the gel surface evenly. Wait 5 minutes — you'll watch the gel turn into a solid, peelable sheet. Then flip it (carefully!) and spray the back. Wait another 5 minutes.

Why it mattersCalcium ions cross-link the alginate strands into a 3D net. This is the moment goo becomes plastic. It's the same chemistry that makes the spheres in molecular gastronomy.
10:00
Cross-link timer Mist · wait · flip · mist · wait.
snap! ⚡
07Rinse and dry

Peel, rinse, and air-dry overnight.

Carefully peel your bioplastic off the dish. Rinse it under cool water for 30 seconds to wash off extra calcium chloride. Lay it flat on parchment or a drying rack. By morning it'll be tougher, slightly stiffer, and ready to keep, cut, or test.

Why it mattersThe drying changes the texture as water leaves the matrix. Tomorrow you can test: Does it tear? Stretch? Float? Burn? (Last one — only with your teacher.)
overnight ☾

The science behind it

One reaction does all the work.

The whole experiment hinges on a single moment: the spray of calcium ions onto the alginate gel. Here's what's actually happening at the molecular level.

Cross-link reaction

Goo to plastic, in 30 seconds.

Sodium alginate is a long, soft chain — like a strand of cooked spaghetti. On its own, it just slides around in water. That's why your bowl had goo, not plastic.

When calcium ions (Ca²⁺) hit the gel, each ion grabs onto two alginate strands and pulls them together. Multiply that by millions of ions and millions of strands, and you've built a 3D net — a cross-linked polymer matrix. That net is what holds your bioplastic together.

Same chemistry, different scales: this reaction is how olive-shaped "caviar" gets its skin in molecular gastronomy. It's how some wound dressings work. And it's how researchers at AltaSea are exploring kelp-based packaging that breaks down in seawater instead of polluting it.

Take it further

Once you've made one, make it weird.

The recipe is a starting point. Real material scientists run dozens of variations to figure out what their material can do. Try these.

Test what it can do

Compare to a real plastic bag. Stretch test, water test, tear test. Make a chart of what you find.

Vary one variable

Double the glycerin. Halve the alginate. Try less water. Run the recipe again and write down what changes.

Watch it break down

Bury one piece in soil. Float one in tap water. Float one in salt water. Photograph daily for a week.

Make a real object

Mold it into a bag, a sticker, a sheet for wrapping a sandwich. Use it once. Did it work? Why?

Time the reaction

How fast does cross-linking happen with hotter water? Colder? Stronger calcium chloride? You're a chemist now.

Pitch a use case

What would you use this material for? Write a one-page pitch and share it with your class. Best ideas spread.
