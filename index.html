Why this game

Three things you'll do that no textbook lets you do.

01

You build a real food web.

Lay out organism cards on the ocean board. Connect them with arrows: who eats who, who eats what. By the end of Phase 1, you've made a living map of the whole kelp forest.

02

You see what breaks it.

Flip an "Uh Oh" card. Maybe sharks are gone. Maybe the water's getting warmer. Watch your food web start to fall apart, piece by piece.

03

You figure out how to fix it.

Play a "Fix It" card. Protect predators. Start a kelp farm. Cut pollution. Try to bring your ocean back to balance before the round ends.

How a round works

Four phases, one ecosystem.

Each round of Tangled Tides moves through these four phases. The whole game takes about 30–45 minutes, and most classes play three rounds.

Phase01 ~10 min

Build your ocean.

Place every organism card on the ocean board. Kelp goes deep, sea lions stay up top. Then connect them with strings or arrows: who eats what.

By the time you finish, the whole web should be visible at a glance: sun → kelp → urchins → sheephead → sea bass → sea lion. With side branches for lobsters, garibaldi, and the rest.

Phase02 ~10–15 min

Uh Oh cards. Now break it.

Flip an Uh Oh card. Watch the food web react. Then play a Fix It card and try to bring it back to balance.

Example cards: "Too many sea urchins!" · "Predators are gone!" · "Water is getting warmer!"

🚧 WIP

Interactive food web simulator

This is where the digital companion to Phase 2 lives. Click an Uh Oh card, watch the SVG ecosystem react in real time. Click a Fix It card, watch it recover. Health meter, round counter, the works. Coming next iteration.

Phase03 ~10–15 min

Fix it challenge.

This is where your team becomes the ecosystem managers. You've watched things break in Phase 2. Now figure out which Fix It cards undo the damage — and in what order.

Some fixes are obvious. Some aren't. "Reduce pollution" works for warming water but doesn't bring sharks back. "Protect predators" stops urchins but takes time to show up in the kelp. Real ocean managers think this way every day.

Phase04 ~5–10 min

Quick reflection.

Four questions. Short answers. Not a quiz — a way to lock in what just happened.

  • What happened when kelp disappeared?
  • What caused the biggest problem?
  • Which Fix It worked best, and why?
  • Why does kelp matter — to fish, to people, to the planet?
The science behind it

One missing link can collapse the whole web.

This isn't just a game design choice. It's how real ecosystems actually work.

Apex🦭 sea lion
Predator🐟 sheephead · bass
Grazer🟣 urchins
Producer🌿 giant kelp

The keystone test.

In Catalina's kelp forests, California sheephead are the keystone species — the one that holds everything else up. Their teeth crush sea urchins. Without sheephead, urchins explode. Without controls, urchins eat all the kelp. Without kelp, every other species loses its home.

One link breaks. Everything else follows. This is called a trophic cascade, and California has lost up to 95% of its kelp forests in some areas because of exactly this kind of chain reaction.

Tangled Tides lets students see that chain reaction in 10 minutes, on a board, with cards in their hands. Then they get to try to stop it.

Take it further

Once you've played one round, make it harder.

🏆

Run all 3 rounds

Same ecosystem, different Uh Oh card each round. Track which fixes worked best. Build a class leaderboard.

⏱

Time it

Race teams on which can rebalance fastest. Fast doesn't always mean stable — discuss why after.

🔍

Research a species

Pick one organism card. Find out where it actually lives, what it eats, and what threatens it. Add a note to the card.

✏️

Make your own Uh Oh card

What's a real disruption you've read about? Plastic pollution? Oil spills? Write your own card and share with the class.

📣

Pitch a real fix

If your team had a real-world budget, what would you spend it on? Write a one-page proposal to your local government.

🌊

Visit a real one

AltaSea, Cabrillo Marine Aquarium, or the Catalina coast all let you see kelp forests in real life. Field trip incoming.
