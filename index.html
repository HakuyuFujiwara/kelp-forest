Field protocol

How a round runs.

The physical kit mirrors what the simulator does. Students place organism cards on a board, draw arrows for predation, then play disruption and intervention cards in sequence. Most classrooms repeat the loop three times — same starting ecosystem, different perturbations each round.

// 01 Build your ocean. Place every organism card on the board. Draw arrows for who eats whom. By the end the food web is visible at a glance.
// 02 “Uh Oh” phase. Flip a disruption card and remove or boost the affected species. Watch the cascade play out in the simulator above.
// 03 Fix It challenge. Pick an intervention. Some match the disruption; others don't. Discover which by trying.
// 04 Reflect. What collapsed, what caused the worst damage, which fix worked, and what humans can do for real kelp forests.

About this model

This is a simplified simulation of a Southern California giant kelp forest ecosystem (e.g. Channel Islands, Catalina Island), designed to teach trophic cascades and ecosystem dynamics aligned with NGSS MS-LS2 standards.

Real-world basis

The species and interactions in this simulation are grounded in published kelp forest research:

  • Sheephead & spiny lobster as the primary urchin predators reflects real Southern California ecology — sea otters, the more famous urchin predator, are largely absent south of Point Conception. (Hamilton & Caselle 2015; Eisaguirre et al. 2020)
  • Urchin barrens — large areas where urchins overgraze and eliminate kelp — are a real and growing phenomenon along the California coast since 2014. (Smith et al. 2021, PNAS)
  • The thermal anomaly disruption is modeled on the 2014–2016 Northeast Pacific marine heatwave, which devastated California kelp forests. (McPherson et al. 2021)
  • The predator collapse disruption echoes the sea star wasting disease epidemic (2013–present) that functionally extirpated Pycnopodia helianthoides, a key urchin predator. (Surma et al. 2025)

Important simplifications

To make the cascade visible and learnable, this model takes some liberties with reality:

  • The food web is collapsed into a four-level food chain. In reality sea bass and sea lions eat many prey species; this model isolates one pathway for clarity.
  • Cascade strength is exaggerated. Recent research suggests larval recruitment, oceanographic regimes, and substrate complexity may matter as much as predation in controlling urchin populations. (Malakhoff & Miller 2021; Langendorf et al. 2025)
  • Population dynamics use logistic-style equations with prey-dependent carrying capacity. Real populations also depend on age structure, larval supply, disease, and stochastic events not modeled here.
  • Time is abstract — one "tick" is roughly seasonal, but populations respond faster than they would in nature so students can see results in minutes, not decades.

Learning goals (NGSS MS-LS2)

  • MS-LS2-1 — Effects of resource availability on populations (kelp ↔ urchin, urchin ↔ predators).
  • MS-LS2-2 — Patterns of predator-prey interaction across trophic levels.
  • MS-LS2-4 — How disruptions to one component affect all populations.
  • MS-LS2-5 — Evaluating intervention strategies (MPAs, restoration, climate policy) for maintaining biodiversity.

Designed as an instructional tool. For real research data, see Channel Islands National Park kelp monitoring program and PISCO long-term reef surveys.
